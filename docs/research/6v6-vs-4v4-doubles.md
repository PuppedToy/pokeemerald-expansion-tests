**Our doubles are 6v6.** The VGC corpus is 4v4 (bring-6/pick-4). This table (from the DOU 6v6 corpus)
is the lens for deciding which VGC conclusions apply to us. Owner-validated conclusions go in the
rating-decisions log, not here.

| Aspect | VGC (4v4) | Smogon DOU (6v6) | Implication for our 6v6 game |
|---|---|---|---|
| Entry hazards (Stealth Rock / Spikes) | Effectively absent. With bring-4 and short (~10-15 turn) games, forced switches are rare, so Stealth Rock/Spikes chip almost never accumulates, and spending an action to set a hazard rarely pays off. Removal (Defog/Rapid Spin) is likewise skipped. The 'hazards are dead in doubles' belief is a 4v4 artifact. | Present and meaningful, though light. Several verified sample teams run a single Stealth Rock (Landorus-T on the SM 'Metagross Tailwind' and 'Scizor Balance' builds, Kommo-o on Charizard-Y Sun) precisely because 6v6 has far more forced switching (VoltTurn pivoting, Shadow Tag) for the chip to punish. Removal even earns a slot: the Sun team runs Landorus-T Defog specifically to keep SR off 4x-weak Charizard. It is one SR layer as a momentum tax, not the multi-layer Spikes stacking of singles. | Do not treat hazards as dead — that conclusion is 4v4-biased. In our 6v6 game SR chip and hazard removal have real value because switching is common; rate/balance Stealth Rock, Defog and Rapid Spin as meaningful momentum tools (roughly a singles-lite level), not as unusable filler. |
| Switching & pivoting | Limited. Each of only 4 Pokemon is 25% of the team, so hard-commits and double-targets dominate; U-turn/Volt Switch are used mostly to reposition or re-apply Intimidate for a turn, not to build momentum over many turns. | Central. Two extra bodies make pivoting, sacking and cycling affordable. Nearly every sample team carries a U-turn pivot (Landorus-T on the great majority) plus Volt Switch (Tapu Koko), and the teams explicitly note U-turn 'pivots into Diancie/Kyurem-B safely (a 6v6 switching tool absent in VGC bring-6/pick-4).' Regenerator glue and Shadow Tag trapping both exist specifically to exploit the switch. | Treat switching as a first-class mechanic. Pivot moves (U-turn/Volt Switch), Intimidate re-application, Regenerator, trapping and hazards all gain value in 6v6. Team-building and AI should reward momentum cores rather than static 4-mon packages. |
| Team size / win-condition count (bring-6 vs bring-4) | Bring 6, pick 4 after Team Preview. You tailor a tight 4-Pokemon package to each matchup and run essentially one win condition; with only 4 slots there is no room for backup setters or single-purpose support mons — every slot must pay off immediately. | All 6 are brought and used. Win conditions are spread across the roster and redundancy is a feature: the Mega Diancie build lists Diancie, Kyurem-B, WP Aegislash and Talonflame as independent late-game closers, and the Trick Room teams carry THREE TR setters (Stakataka/Diancie/Porygon2) so the mode survives losing one. Teams can afford dedicated support-only mons (a pure Follow Me Jirachi, a pure trapper Gothitelle). | In 6v6, reward depth and redundancy — multiple independent closers, backup speed-control/TR setters and dedicated support mons are correct, not wasted slots. Balancing/AI should value 6-mon plans with a late-game win condition reachable through sacking, not a single tight 4-mon core. |
| Redirection (Follow Me / Rage Powder) | Premium and near-mandatory. In a 4-mon game, Amoonguss/Togekiss Rage Powder/Follow Me protecting the win condition for even one turn is often game-deciding, so redirection is one of the most valued support roles in the format. | Strong and common too (Amoonguss Rage Powder appears on several teams; Jirachi Follow Me anchors the SemiRoom build), but the longer game gives opponents more turns and bodies to route around it — Substitute (Volcanion, Camerupt 'plays around Fake Out / Amoonguss redirection'), spread moves that hit through it, Taunt, and simply switching. It is one tool among Wide Guard, Intimidate and speed control rather than the sole shield. | Keep redirection a strong support archetype but ensure counterplay exists (Substitute, spread moves, Taunt, switching). In 6v6 it should be valuable without being as mandatory as it is in 4v4. |
| Speed control (Tailwind / Trick Room / Icy Wind / Choice Scarf) | The defining teambuilding constraint — almost every team runs Tailwind, Trick Room, Icy Wind or Prankster support. But short games usually grant one window and one mode; a team commits to fast (Tailwind) or slow (TR), rarely both. | Equally essential but built with redundancy: teams stack two independent tools so pace survives losing one — 'Talonflame Tailwind + Choice Scarf Landorus-T,' 'redundant Tailwind from Suicune AND Latios,' Kartana Tailwind + Porygon2 Icy Wind backup, THREE TR setters on the Room teams, and even semi-room builds that keep both a fast and a TR plan. Choice Scarf revenge-killers (Scarf Landorus-T recurs) are a DOU speed-control staple VGC uses far less. | Speed control is the backbone in 6v6 too, but value REDUNDANT speed control and Choice Scarf revenge-killers, and let TR teams run multiple setters. Rate Tailwind/Trick Room/Icy Wind and Choice Scarf highly — a 6v6 team wants a second layer, not a single window. |
| Weather | A viable archetype but usually one auto-weather setter defining a short game; with only 4 bodies you rarely re-establish weather after the setter falls, and duration items matter less over few turns. | A full archetype with support to sustain and exploit it: Smooth Rock/Damp Rock extend duration, weather doubles Speed as its own speed control (Excadrill Sand Rush, Swampert/Ludicolo Swift Swim), the 6-mon shell protects and re-sets the setter, and — uniquely — Gothitelle Shadow Tag traps and deletes the bulky-Water/Grass checks that wall rain. Long-game weather-vs-weather wars reward the deeper build. | Support weather as a strong 6v6 archetype: value duration rocks (Smooth/Damp Rock), weather-Speed abilities (Swift Swim/Sand Rush) as speed control, and the ability to protect/re-establish weather. Tune weather duration and setter access for a long game, not a 4-turn burst. |
| Protect usage | Near-universal — most Pokemon run Protect. In a 4-mon spread-move game it scouts, stalls Tailwind/TR/weather timers, dodges double-targeting and preserves scarce bodies; it is the single most common move in VGC. | Still a staple (nearly every offensive mon in the samples carries Protect), but marginally less universal: more spare bodies plus alternative shields (Wide Guard, redirection, Substitute) cover the same job, and utility/attacker slots often skip it — Assault Vest Landorus-T, Choice-locked mons, and Fake-Out Kangaskhan sets run four other moves. | Protect stays a staple in 6v6, but also value Wide/Quick Guard, Substitute and redirection as alternative protection, and allow AV/Choice sets that forgo it. Don't assume every mon runs Protect when rating movesets or building teams. |
| Item choices | Short games favor immediate or single-use payoffs: Focus Sash, pinch berries (Sitrus/Figy/Iapapa/Mago), Life Orb, Assault Vest, plus era Mega Stones / Z-crystals and Safety Goggles. A one-time berry proc or a Sash is often enough to swing a short game; recurring items matter less. | Those still appear, but longevity and recurring value shine more over the long game — Leftovers, Assault Vest, Eviolite (Porygon2), Weakness Policy, and duration rocks (Smooth/Damp Rock). Choice Scarf as a speed-control/revenge item is far more common than in VGC (Scarf Landorus-T recurs). Neither modern format enforces an item clause, so duplicates are legal in both; the difference is which items are good. | Balance items for both regimes: in 6v6, longevity items (Leftovers/AV/Eviolite), Choice Scarf revenge-killers and weather-extension rocks gain value alongside the VGC-standard Sash/berry/Life Orb. Don't tune item value purely for 4v4 short-game bursts. |
| Bulky pivots / Regenerator | Useful but capped — Amoonguss/Tornadus/Slowking see play (Amoonguss is a VGC staple), but the heal-on-switch payoff is limited by how few switch cycles a 4-mon short game allows; Regenerator is often just a bonus on a mon brought mainly for redirection/Spore. | Much stronger — the long game lets a Regenerator pivot switch in, absorb a hit or redirect, leave, and return healthy many times; the samples describe Amoonguss as a 'Regenerator pivot that pivots in and out repeatedly across a long 6v6 game.' It pairs naturally with U-turn/Volt Switch cores. | Regenerator and bulky pivots are premium in 6v6 because heal-on-switch scales with game length and switch frequency. Rate Regenerator and pivoting cores highly; they are a distinctly-6v6 defensive backbone, not the marginal role they are in 4v4. |
| Trapping (Shadow Tag / Arena Trap) | Essentially absent — Shadow Tag (Gothitelle, Wobbuffet) and Arena Trap are banned in modern VGC, and even unbanned, the few switches in a short 4-mon game would sharply limit their value. | A signature 6v6 mechanic. Two full sample teams (Gothitelle Rain, Scizor/Electric-spam Balance) are built on Gothitelle Shadow Tag, which traps and deletes the specific answer to the plan (rain checks, bulky Waters, Chansey) — a play 'only possible because 6v6 DOU rewards preventing switches.' Legal in Smogon DOU under its own ban list. | Trapping is a powerful, distinctly-6v6 archetype (remove-the-counter-then-sweep). Decide deliberately whether to allow and how to balance Shadow Tag/Arena Trap; it shapes 6v6 in a way it never does in 4v4, so it should not be dismissed on VGC grounds. |
| Intimidate & Fake Out disruption | Cornerstones — Incineroar (Intimidate + Fake Out) is a defining VGC Pokemon, and stacking Intimidate to blunt physical offense is standard practice. | Also cornerstones, and if anything re-applied more: double Intimidate (Incineroar + Landorus-T) is a stock backbone, and U-turn pivoting lets a team re-trigger Intimidate repeatedly over the long game ('Landorus-T repeatedly re-applies Intimidate by pivoting — true switching that VGC's short games rarely reward'). Fake Out buys the turns to set TR/Tailwind/trapping. | This is more agreement than difference — value Intimidate and Fake Out highly in both. The 6v6 wrinkle is that pivoting re-applies Intimidate, so Intimidate + U-turn users are especially strong; weight those combinations accordingly. |
| Spread moves & Wide/Quick Guard | Spread moves (Rock Slide, Heat Wave, Blizzard, Muddy Water) and their 0.75x multiplier are core, and Wide Guard is a common, valued answer; friendly-fire from spread moves shapes target selection and Protect timing. | Same dynamics, but the longer game makes dedicated Wide Guard users (Aegislash, Conkeldurr) a reliable recurring shield rather than a one-off; the samples pair Wide Guard with Intimidate to blanket spread offense and flag friendly-fire risk (ally-hitting Rock Slide vs foe-only Diamond Storm). | Spread moves and Wide/Quick Guard matter in both formats; in 6v6 a dedicated Wide Guard user is a durable, repeatable support role. Model spread-move friendly-fire correctly and value Wide/Quick Guard as genuine support, not niche tech. |
| Game length / attrition & sacrificing | Short and burst-oriented — you race to convert your 4-mon plan before timers (Tailwind/TR/weather) lapse. Attrition, chip and sacking a body to preserve a win condition are minor because there is little game left to grind. | Long and attrition-friendly — chip (hazards, Life Orb, weather/sand), recovery (Recover/Roost/Regenerator), and deliberately sacking a body to bring in a closer safely all matter; multiple sample teams win by grinding bulk down over many turns and cashing a late-game sweep. | In 6v6, value attrition tools (recovery, chip, longevity) and plan for late-game win conditions and deliberate sacking. Balancing should not assume the burst-y short game of 4v4; endurance and recovery are rewarded over many turns. |
