## Synergies (24)

### Redirection shields a fragile setup / restricted (Follow Me & Rage Powder) _(redirection (doubles))_
In doubles, Follow Me / Rage Powder force every single-target enemy attack onto the user, buying one fully protected turn for a fragile partner to boost, fire a two-turn move, or set up a restricted. It works because the opponent cannot ignore the redirect without spread moves, Safety Goggles, or Sub-breaking chip; the redirector is usually bulky, Regenerator, or Sitrus-healed so it can eat the redirected hits repeatedly.

_Examples:_ Follow Me — Se Jun Park's 2014 World Champion team; The 'Big 6' — the format-defining VGC 2016 hyper-offense archetype; James Baek — 'TornOgre History' (Xerneas / Primal Kyogre rain offense)

### Draw-in immunity abilities that redirect AND cover a partner's weakness _(ability redirection (doubles))_
Lightning Rod / Storm Drain / Volt Absorb pull a whole move type onto an immune body, so the same slot both redirects and papers over a teammate's crippling weakness — letting a 4x-weak restricted or mega sit on the field safely. Telepathy variants dodge the partner's own spread move.

_Examples:_ Wolfe Glick — 2016 World Champion (Kyogre/Rayquaza + Lightning Rod Raichu); Paul Ruiz — 2018 World Champion ("Soaring Higher"); Follow Me — Se Jun Park's 2014 World Champion team

### Weather + weather-abuser (speed and power) _(weather)_
A permanent-weather setter (Drizzle/Drought) simultaneously boosts matching STAB and switches on a partner's Swift Swim/Chlorophyll to double its Speed, turning a mid-speed attacker into a fast nuke. Rain also ~doubles Water damage and makes Thunder/Hurricane 100% accurate; sun makes Solar Beam instant. Common to both doubles and singles rain/sun.

_Examples:_ What's Protect? — Alex Ogloza's US Nationals Champion team; Manaphy Rain (Ske Rain); 'Dancing in the Sun' — Torkoal / Lilligant sun offense

### Weather as defense / weather denial _(weather)_
The same weather that boosts you also erases a category of enemy damage or a teammate weakness: Primordial Sea/rain nullifies Fire, Desolate Land nullifies Water, and setting your own weather overwrites the opponent's — shutting off their Swift Swim/Chlorophyll and their STAB boosts.

_Examples:_ Wolfe Glick — 2016 World Champion (Kyogre/Rayquaza + Lightning Rod Raichu); Shoma Honami's World Champion team ("shadeVIERA"); What's Protect? — Alex Ogloza's US Nationals Champion team

### Trick Room + slow, hyper-bulky attackers _(trick room / speed control)_
Trick Room inverts the speed order for five turns so deliberately-slow, tanky attackers (Aegislash, Snorlax, Heatran, Stakataka, Adamant Primal Groudon) move first and hit hardest while surviving anything. Setters are chosen to be bulky and Fake-Out-immune so they live to flip the field.

_Examples:_ Hideyuki Taida's Worlds Runner-Up team ("BIDC"); Naoto Mizobuchi — 2019 World Champion (Primal Groudon / Lunala Trick Room); Shoma Honami's World Champion team ("shadeVIERA")

### Dual / redundant Trick Room setters for reliability _(trick room / speed control)_
Two independent TR setters make the mode land against Taunt, Fake Out, and focus-fire that would stop a lone setter — you can lose one and still invert speed. The cost is bench slots, but it makes an otherwise all-or-nothing plan dependable.

_Examples:_ Aaron Traylor — 2016 Worlds Top 8 (Dual-setter Trick Room Xerneas/Groudon); Naoto Mizobuchi — 2019 World Champion (Primal Groudon / Lunala Trick Room)

### Intimidate stacking _(intimidate (doubles))_
Two or more Intimidate bodies that pivot in and out (Incineroar, Landorus-T, Mega Salamence) repeatedly drop enemy Attack, compounding to neutralize physical offense and protect fragile setup sweepers turn after turn. Primarily a doubles engine, where multiple Intimidate switch-ins are practical.

_Examples:_ Paul Ruiz — 2018 World Champion ("Soaring Higher"); Zheyuan 'Ken' Huang — 'The best Xerndon' (Xerneas / Primal Groudon); 20XX Mence (Alberto Lara / CaliSweeper)

### Fake Out into free setup / tempo _(tempo (doubles))_
Fake Out flinches one foe for a guaranteed free turn a partner spends on Belly Drum / Dragon Dance / Geomancy, or that the team uses for positioning in place of Protect. It stacks value when carried on an Intimidate body (Incineroar), giving damage reduction and tempo in one slot.

_Examples:_ Paul Ruiz — 2018 World Champion ("Soaring Higher"); Nils Dunlop — 2018 Worlds Top 4 (Mega Metagross balance); What's Protect? — Alex Ogloza's US Nationals Champion team

### Layered non-weather speed control (Tailwind / paralysis / Icy Wind / Electroweb) _(speed control)_
Stacking speed control lets a slower core move first without committing to Trick Room: Tailwind doubles team Speed for four turns, Thunder Wave/Nuzzle paralysis halves a threat's Speed, and Icy Wind/Electroweb/Rock Tomb drop enemy Speed while chipping. Often a redundant second layer behind the primary plan.

_Examples:_ Follow Me — Se Jun Park's 2014 World Champion team; Paul Ruiz — 2018 World Champion ("Soaring Higher"); Raghav 'Mudhiman' Malaviya — 'RayOgre's Evolution' (Mega Rayquaza / Primal Kyogre)

### Priority + anti-priority axis (Gale Wings / Bullet Punch + Quick/Wide Guard) _(priority axis (doubles))_
Fast offense pairs a priority finisher (Gale Wings Brave Bird, Bullet Punch, Extreme Speed) to pick off weakened or faster threats with Quick Guard / Wide Guard to block the opponent's priority and spread moves on the turns it matters — controlling both ends of the priority axis.

_Examples:_ When It Rains, It Pours (Jack Hatch / Logilink); Follow Me — Se Jun Park's 2014 World Champion team; The 'Big 6' — the format-defining VGC 2016 hyper-offense archetype

### Screens / Aurora Veil + setup sweepers _(screens)_
Aurora Veil (or Reflect + Light Screen) halves incoming damage for 5–8 turns with Light Clay, converting fragile boosters' setup turns into guaranteed survival so they snowball behind the screen. Snow Warning Ninetales sets Veil in a single slot.

_Examples:_ Sam Pandelis — 2017 Worlds Runner-up (Aurora Veil defensive setup)

### Terrain + terrain abuser _(terrain)_
A Surge ability both boosts matching STAB and unlocks a partner mechanic: Electric Terrain triggers Electric Seed (+Def) and Unburden; Grassy Terrain heals grounded allies and halves Earthquake; Psychic Terrain blocks priority into a setup; Misty Terrain blocks status. A Gen 7+ staple in both formats.

_Examples:_ Kokolucha Hyper Offense (Xtra HO / Medi Koko Lucha HO); Ash-Greninja Balance; Sam Pandelis — 2017 Worlds Runner-up (Aurora Veil defensive setup)

### Hazard stacking + wallbreakers _(hazards (singles))_
Stacking Stealth Rock + Spikes chips every switch-in so wallbreakers cross KO thresholds they otherwise couldn't and revenge-killers finish through Sashes/Sturdy. A one-sided hazard game pairs stacking with your own Defog so only the opponent eats the chip. Largely a singles pattern.

_Examples:_ Ash-Greninja Balance; Manaphy Rain (Ske Rain); Speed-Boosting Blacephalon Hyper Offense

### Trapping to remove a specific answer (Shadow Tag / Pursuit / Magma Storm) _(trapping)_
A trapper deletes the one Pokémon that walls your win condition: Shadow Tag holds it in to be KO'd or Perished, Pursuit punishes the forced switch of Psychic/Ghost pivots, Magma Storm chip-locks. This surgically opens a sweep or a Perish/2v2 endgame.

_Examples:_ KeldTar Bulky Offense; Emilio Forbes — 2018 Worlds Runner-up (Mega Gengar Perish Trap); Follow Me — Se Jun Park's 2014 World Champion team

### VoltTurn / pivoting momentum _(pivoting)_
U-turn / Volt Switch (and Parting Shot) deal damage while switching, letting a bulky pivot bring a frail breaker in for free and keep initiative. In singles it wins the momentum war for Choice-locked breakers; in doubles it also repositions the active pair.

_Examples:_ Mega Medicham Offense; Kokolucha Hyper Offense (Xtra HO / Medi Koko Lucha HO); Ash-Greninja Balance

### Regenerator pivot core _(defensive pivot)_
Two or three Regenerator bodies (Toxapex, Tornadus-T, Amoonguss, Slowbro) recover ~33% every time they pivot out, so they can check the tier repeatedly and reset chip without spending recovery turns — the sustainable backbone of balance in both formats.

_Examples:_ Ash-Greninja Balance; Swords Dance Mega Mawile Bulky Offense (Sand); James Baek — 'TornOgre History' (Xerneas / Primal Kyogre rain offense)

### Physical/special wall split + cleric/Wish recovery engine _(stall (singles))_
Full stall pairs a dedicated physical wall with a dedicated special wall so nothing hits an unresisted hole, then keeps them alive with a shared recovery/status-cure engine (Wish-passing, Regenerator, Soft-Boiled, Heal Bell/Natural Cure) and an Unaware body to blank setup.

_Examples:_ Mega Aggron Stall; Mega Sableye Stall (The Great Walls)

### Magic Bounce + spin (two-way hazard control) _(hazard control (singles))_
Mega Sableye's Magic Bounce reflects the opponent's hazards, Taunt and status straight back, while a spinner lays and clears on your own side — so stall wins the hazard war it normally fears and stacks its own Toxic Spikes at the same time.

_Examples:_ Mega Sableye Stall (The Great Walls)

### Anti-setup glue (Unaware / Haze / phazing) _(anti-setup)_
Control answers to enemy boosting: Unaware ignores the foe's stat changes, Haze wipes all boosts, and phazing (Roar/Whirlwind/Dragon Tail) forces the boosted mon out — often into hazard chip. Essential to beat the setup sweepers offense relies on.

_Examples:_ Mega Aggron Stall; Ash-Greninja Balance; Speed-Boosting Blacephalon Hyper Offense

### Complementary offensive core / unavoidable spread pressure _(offensive core)_
Two attackers whose STABs together hit the tier for at least neutral, with defensive synergy on the back end — Water + Fairy, Ground/Fire + Fairy, Fighting-spam. In doubles the same idea appears as spread pressure (Heat Wave + Hyper Voice) that damages both foes at once and can't be Protected away by one target.

_Examples:_ Zheyuan 'Ken' Huang — 'The best Xerndon' (Xerneas / Primal Groudon); 20XX Mence (Alberto Lara / CaliSweeper); Mega Medicham Offense

### Healing Wish / Wish support for frail breakers _(support (singles))_
A support mon sacrifices itself (Healing Wish) or passes Wish to fully restore a chipped, recovery-less wallbreaker so it can break a second time — turning a one-shot glass cannon into a repeatable threat.

_Examples:_ Mega Medicham Offense

### Dancer copies setup (free second sweeper) _(setup copy (doubles))_
Dancer (Oricorio) auto-copies any dance move a teammate uses, so one Quiver Dance boosts two sweepers at once — snowballing two win conditions off a single setup turn.

_Examples:_ 'Dancing in the Sun' — Torkoal / Lilligant sun offense

### Damage-suppression control making a tank un-KOable (Intimidate + Snarl + Misty) _(damage suppression (doubles))_
Stacking Attack drops (Intimidate), Special-Attack drops (Snarl) and status-blocking terrain lowers both offensive categories so a bulky/Assault-Vest attacker effectively cannot be KO'd while it chips the whole enemy team — slowing the game into your favor.

_Examples:_ AFK / 'FAKEPG' core (Arcanine-Tapu Fini-Kartana bulky balance); Zheyuan 'Ken' Huang — 'The best Xerndon' (Xerneas / Primal Groudon)

### Anti-engine disruption (Taunt + screen/setup removal) _(disruption)_
Taunt shuts off Geomancy, Trick Room, Tailwind, recovery and hazards; Brick Break / screen removal shatters Aurora Veil. Bringing both denies the opponent's engine before it starts and is the standard counter to defensive-setup and setup-lead teams.

_Examples:_ Ryota Otsubo — 2017 World Champion (dual-Z Whimsicott tempo offense); Wolfe Glick — 2016 World Champion (Kyogre/Rayquaza + Lightning Rod Raichu)

## Anti-synergies (21)

### Two Mega Stones — only one Mega per battle
A team carrying two mega stones can only Mega-Evolve one Pokémon per game, so half the mega investment sits on the bench every match and the 'other' mega is played as a weaker base form or a pure bluff. It buys matchup flexibility at the cost of a dead slot each game.

_Examples:_ Charizard the Benchwarmer — starmetroid's sun team; 20XX Mence (Alberto Lara / CaliSweeper); The 'Big 6' — the format-defining VGC 2016 hyper-offense archetype

### Competing weathers overwrite each other
Two weather setters — especially two restricteds like Primordial Sea Kyogre + Desolate Land Groudon, or Mega Rayquaza's Delta Stream — cancel each other's field, so their boosts are mutually exclusive and fielding both together dilutes each. They are really two separate game plans, not a combo.

_Examples:_ Jonathan 'Ezrael' Evans — 2016 Worlds Finalist (Double Primal); Wolfe Glick — 2016 World Champion (Kyogre/Rayquaza + Lightning Rod Raichu); Raghav 'Mudhiman' Malaviya — 'RayOgre's Evolution' (Mega Rayquaza / Primal Kyogre)

### Weather-neutral win condition ignores (or hates) the team's weather
On a dual-win-con team where one condition wants weather and the other is weather-neutral or hurt by it, the two pull opposite directions — the 'off' piece is dead investment that game, and its coverage may even be weakened by the active weather.

_Examples:_ When It Rains, It Pours (Jack Hatch / Logilink); Charizard the Benchwarmer — starmetroid's sun team

### Fast mode vs Trick Room mode tension
Carrying both fast pieces and a Trick Room setter forces an all-in choice each game: commit to TR and the fast attackers are stranded moving last; commit to fast and the TR setter and slow attackers are dead weight. If both modes come up at once they actively undercut each other.

_Examples:_ Tomohiro Seki — 2018 Worlds Top 8 (Cresselia Trick Room dual-terrain balance); Nils Dunlop — 2018 Worlds Top 4 (Mega Metagross balance); Naoto Mizobuchi — 2019 World Champion (Primal Groudon / Lunala Trick Room)

### Shared type-weakness stacking
Multiple members sharing a weakness let one attacker or coverage move threaten several at once, and stacking same-type attackers hands their common check a free wall. The team gets punished by a single well-chosen coverage move.

_Examples:_ Rain Offense (Pelipper + Mega Swampert + Ash-Greninja); Kokolucha Hyper Offense (Xtra HO / Medi Koko Lucha HO); Mega Charizard X Hyper Offense

### Attacker-spam walled by one shared check
Two attackers of the same offensive type overwhelm nothing if a single bulky answer resists both — Fighting-spam collapses into a bulky Psychic/Fairy — so the whole offense can be stonewalled unless a trapper removes that check first.

_Examples:_ Mega Medicham Offense; KeldTar Bulky Offense

### 4x or crippling weakness on a linchpin
A key piece with a 4x weakness (or a crippling single weakness) is a pressure point the opponent aims at, forcing the team to spend a whole slot (Shuca Berry, a dedicated redirector) papering over it — and if that patch is removed, the linchpin folds.

_Examples:_ Shoma Honami's World Champion team ("shadeVIERA"); Follow Me — Se Jun Park's 2014 World Champion team; Swords Dance Mega Mawile Bulky Offense (Sand)

### Single point of failure (centralized enabler)
When one enabler — weather setter, redirector, screen setter, sole hazard setter — props up the entire plan, removing it collapses the team. The more centralized the engine, the more it inverts into a liability the opponent can target directly.

_Examples:_ What's Protect? — Alex Ogloza's US Nationals Champion team; 'Dancing in the Sun' — Torkoal / Lilligant sun offense; Sam Pandelis — 2017 Worlds Runner-up (Aurora Veil defensive setup)

### No Protect / no scouting in doubles
Forgoing Protect across a doubles team removes the tool used to scout, dodge double-targeting, stall out Trick Room/Tailwind turns, and pivot safely — leaving the team acutely exposed to spread damage and focus-fire even as it wins the raw-tempo race.

_Examples:_ What's Protect? — Alex Ogloza's US Nationals Champion team

### No hazard removal on a hazard-vulnerable offense
A frail, switch-heavy offense with no Defog/Rapid Spin (or only a fragile one) is ground down by Stealth Rock + Spikes across its many forced switches — especially recovery-less breakers that can't afford the chip. A singles failure mode.

_Examples:_ KeldTar Bulky Offense; Kokolucha Hyper Offense (Xtra HO / Medi Koko Lucha HO); Mega Medicham Offense

### Redundant roles / duplicated tools
Two members doing the same job waste a slot that could patch a weakness — two Focus Sash suicide leads, double Defog, overlapping win conditions. Redundancy is only good when it is deliberate insurance (dual TR setters); here it is just wasted coverage.

_Examples:_ Speed-Boosting Blacephalon Hyper Offense; Mega Aggron Stall

### Choice-lock exploitability
Choice items lock the holder into one move, so a well-timed switch to an immune or resistant pivot earns a free turn and momentum; stacking several Choice users multiplies the read risk and lets bulky pivots dance around the team.

_Examples:_ KeldTar Bulky Offense; Emilio Forbes — 2018 Worlds Runner-up (Mega Gengar Perish Trap); Paul Ruiz — 2018 World Champion ("Soaring Higher")

### Conditional win condition the opponent can decline
A plan that needs the opponent to cooperate — land a super-effective hit (Weakness Policy), stay trapped in, or forgo Safety Goggles — can simply be played around by a careful pilot, so the payoff never comes online.

_Examples:_ Hideyuki Taida's Worlds Runner-Up team ("BIDC"); Follow Me — Se Jun Park's 2014 World Champion team

### Redirection / Sub-trap broken by spread, Snarl, or Safety Goggles
Redirection and Substitute-based trapping are defeated by spread moves that hit through the redirect, by Snarl/multi-hit chip that breaks the Substitute keeping a trap online, or by Safety Goggles that ignore powder — and opponents pack exactly this tech, so the fragile engine can be dismantled at the wrong moment.

_Examples:_ Emilio Forbes — 2018 Worlds Runner-up (Mega Gengar Perish Trap); Follow Me — Se Jun Park's 2014 World Champion team

### Passive control/stall outraced by hyper offense
Slow grind plans (stall, Perish Trap, damage-suppression balance) can be overwhelmed before they stabilize if the opponent simply KOs through them — elite wallbreakers, Taunt, Mold Breaker hazard-setters, and all-out offense race past the control engine.

_Examples:_ Emilio Forbes — 2018 Worlds Runner-up (Mega Gengar Perish Trap); Mega Sableye Stall (The Great Walls); AFK / 'FAKEPG' core (Arcanine-Tapu Fini-Kartana bulky balance)

### Boost-reliant / slow-to-online offense loses the race
Win conditions that need a setup turn (Geomancy, Tail Glow, Belly Drum, Quiver Dance) are dead weight until online — fast Taunt, priority, or being out-sped punishes the tempo cost, and even at +6 a naturally slow sweeper still needs Tailwind/Trick Room to actually move first.

_Examples:_ Sam Pandelis — 2017 Worlds Runner-up (Aurora Veil defensive setup); Paul Ruiz — 2018 World Champion ("Soaring Higher"); James Baek — 'TornOgre History' (Xerneas / Primal Kyogre rain offense)

### Weather war — the opponent's weather flips your abuser
Only one weather can be active, so an opposing setter overwrites yours and instantly turns off Swift Swim/Chlorophyll, kills your STAB boosts, and may even power the foe. Weather offense lives and dies on winning that setter war.

_Examples:_ What's Protect? — Alex Ogloza's US Nationals Champion team; Manaphy Rain (Ske Rain); 'Dancing in the Sun' — Torkoal / Lilligant sun offense

### Weather / terrain is double-edged
A field effect you set also helps the opponent — sun boosts their Fire moves (and burns your own Fire-weak mons), terrain empowers their matching STAB — and weather/terrain timers lapse at inconvenient moments, especially when the setter can't hold an extender item.

_Examples:_ 'Dancing in the Sun' — Torkoal / Lilligant sun offense; Kokolucha Hyper Offense (Xtra HO / Medi Koko Lucha HO)

### Setup-sweeper stack cold to Haze / Unaware / phazing / priority
A team built entirely on boosting sweepers has no fallback against a single Haze/Unaware/phazer or fast priority — one blank of the boosts and the whole win condition evaporates because there is no defensive backbone to buy a reset.

_Examples:_ Speed-Boosting Blacephalon Hyper Offense; The 'Big 6' — the format-defining VGC 2016 hyper-offense archetype

### Incidental weather chips your own team
Weather that isn't fueling a sweeper still deals residual chip to your own non-immune members, partly undoing Regenerator recovery and Leftovers — so the weather quietly works against the team it is on.

_Examples:_ Swords Dance Mega Mawile Bulky Offense (Sand)

### Four-moveslot syndrome / slot tension
A single Pokémon wants more than four moves — STAB, coverage, speed control, and utility — so builders slash options and leave a matchup gap whichever they cut. Support mons trying to do offense + speed control + protection feel it most, and Choice/utility combos strain the slots further.

_Examples:_ When It Rains, It Pours (Jack Hatch / Logilink); KeldTar Bulky Offense; Ash-Greninja Balance
